
### Introduction

The rising rate of osteoarthritis (OA) in young adult patients (i.e., younger than 50 years of age) is a growing concern in orthopaedic health.1-5 A leading precursor to OA is acetabular dysplasia, which is involved in 20–40% of patients with hip OA.6, 7 Additionally, malformations of the hip have also been linked to an increased risk of OA,8 and can manifest from altered mechanical loading and subsequent impingement.9 Damage to soft tissues of the hip, such as the labrum, is highly associated with bony abnormalities of the femur and acetabulum.10, 11 Additionally, bony abnormalities are associated with the majority of young adult patients with hip OA.12 Such bony abnormalities can result from reduced coverage of the proximal femur by the acetabulum (dysplasia) or restricted mobility of the femur in the acetabulum (impingement). Recently, cam-type impingement has been implicated as a major cause of pain in young, active adults and this disorder is now understood to lead to excessive cartilage wear, acetabular delamination, altered hip muscular strength, and eventually OA.13, 14
The role of mechanical stress on joint maturation, health, and disease has been previously elucidated in clinical and epidemiological studies. Acetabular depth and femoral head shape are considered to be mutually dependent.15, 16 The dysplastic hip, which experiences joint laxity and instability, has been characterized as having an elliptical shaped femoral head,16-19 increased femoral neck waist,16 and decreased epiphyseal extension towards the femoral neck.16, 20 Hip dysplasia is well understood to result in increased joint contact stress on the acetabulum and labrum.21-23 However, it is still unclear what drives acetabular or femoral malformations during perinatal growth, and how these pathological adaptations influence cartilage and bone health of the femoroacetabular joint.
To better understand the mechanisms by which pathological adaptations occur in articulating joints, specifically the hip, we and others have explored the normal development of joint formation, growth, and maturation.24-29 It is generally accepted that hip joint formation and maturation depends on a well-orchestrated series of molecular and mechanical events throughout the duration of perinatal development.29-31 In mice, maturation of the proximal femur occurs during postnatal development between 7 and 28 days, when a single chondroepiphysis of the proximal femur discretizes into two epiphyses that separate the femoral head and trochanter.32 The murine hip reaches maturation by 8 weeks of age, indicated by fusion of the triradiate cartilage of the pelvis that connects the ilium, ischium, and pubis. 29 In humans, this maturation occurs during adolescence between 11 and 18 years of age.33 Over the period of a less than two months during postnatal growth, the mouse proximal femur and pelvis start as a relatively soft cartilaginous template and matures into hard, mineralized bone. While the developmental timeline of the musculoskeletal system, in general, is fairly well characterized for mice and humans, the adaptation to loading during periods of postnatal growth of specific hip structures, such as the proximal femur, has received limited attention.
Therefore, a need exists for the development and utilization of small animal models to better understand how mechanical loads during musculoskeletal growth are associated with hip pathology and the development and progression of OA. The use of small animal models, such as mice, may provide insight into the post-natal adaptations involved with joint maturation and instability. To date, most animal models of OA focus on joint injuries to the knee, induced via surgical (destabilization of the medial meniscus34, 35; ligament transection36) or nonsurgical37-39 trauma. While these studies have elucidated the importance of knee joint stability on cartilage health and maintenance, a large gap exists for the study of OA development and progression in load-bearing joints other than the knee.
The purpose of this study was to identify the role of femoroacetabular injury on joint maturation and joint health during linear growth in mice. We hypothesized that altering joint stability and loading prior to joint maturation (i.e., during postnatal growth, prior to triradiate growth plate fusion) via destabilization of the murine hip will cause acetabular dysplasia and focal chondrolabral (i.e., cartilage and labral) degeneration. To investigate this hypothesis, we developed a novel, titrated model of hip instability and induced instability in 3-week old neonatal pups. We compared bone morphometry, histomorphology, shape adaptations, and bone apposition adaptations related to varying levels of hip instability.

## METHODS

Animal Model
All studies were performed in compliance with the Animal Studies Committee at the Department of Comparative Medicine at Washington University. 3-week old C57BL6 mouse pups (N = 42, bred in-house) were used for this study. All surgeries were performed by a board certified orthopaedic surgeon (JCC) using a high-resolution dissection microscope under aseptic conditions. At time of weaning (3-weeks of age), mice (littermates) were assigned to one of four experimental groups (titrated hip instability) or to the naïve control group. The study design and allocation of animals to experimental groups is described in Figure 1. Mice were anesthetized (2–3% isofluorane with 1.5–2% oxygen carry) and subjected to titrated unilateral surgical destabilization via iliacus/iliopsoas tenotomy, capsulotomy, and/or ligamentum teres transection, and instability was confirmed via acute lateralization of the femoral head with the anesthetized mouse lying in supine position. Mice were systematically randomized and assigned to instability groups based on their ordered weight within litters (largest to smallest; alternating assignment to groups). The magnitude of destabilization surgery was consistent within litters (i.e., animals within a given litter received one or two types of instabilities). This approach minimized training effect within a given instability group. A pilot was performed to induce mild, moderate, and severe destabilization (N = 4 for each group). Additional animals were added to moderate and severe groups, with the addition of the femoral head resection (FHR) group, based on findings from the pilot work. For each destabilization, a 1-cm incision was made into the ventral midline of the hip. The leg was placed in hip flexion, abduction, and external rotation and held in place by an assistant. Care was taken not to disturb the neurovascular bundle. For mild destabilization (n = 4 males), a full capsulotomy, iliopsoas tenotomy, and ligamentum teres transection was performed using curved, pointed-tip ophthalmic surgical scissors. Transection of the ligamentum teres was performed after capsulotomy and tenotomy by gentle lateral subluxation for intra-articular exposure of the ligament. For moderate destabilization (n = 10 total; 5 males, 5 males), capsulotomy, iliopsoas tenotomy, and ligamentum teres transection was performed as described for mild instability, and a partial hip abductor tenotomy was also performed by detaching the piriformis, superior and inferior gemellus, and quadratus femoris from its femoral insertion sites. For severe destabilization (n = 12 total; 8 males, 4 females), the same procedure of moderate instability was performed, with full hip abductor tenotomy by detaching the gluteal rotators (gluteus minimus, obturator internus, gluteus maximus, and gluteus medius) from their proximal femoral insertion sites. Femoral head resection (FHR; n = 7 total; 5 males, 2 females) was performed to induce complete unloading of the acetabulum for comparison to destabilization groups. For FHR, capsulotomy, iliopsoas tenotomy, and ligamentum teres transection was performed; however, all other soft tissue attachments were maintained. The femoral head was cut using ophthalmic surgical scissors and carefully removed from the joint space. The naïve control group (n = 9 total; 3 males, 6 females) were age-matched nonoperative controls.

In each destabilization model, the hip was reduced by the surgeon prior to incision closure. Upon completion of soft tissue injuries, the injury site was treated with 0.25% bupivacaine hydrochloride (Marcaine, Hospira, Lake Forest, IL) and the surgical site was closed using 6–0 Vicryl (Ethicon, Somerville, NJ). A sham operation was performed on the contralateral side that included exposure of the hip, identification of the hip joint, and closure of the incision without joint (muscle/ligament) damage. Mice were administered a subcutaneous injection of buprenorphine (Buprenex, Richmond, VA) at a dose of 0.05 mg/kg at time of surgery with no additional doses administered. Following surgery, mice were housed with same-sex littermates (less than 5 per cage) in cages with corn bedding with food and water ad libitum, exposed to a 12 h on/12 h off light/dark cycle, and monitored post-operatively for pain and distress on a daily basis.


 Bone and Joint Morphology
Mice from the mild (N = 3), moderate (N = 6), and severe (N = 6) instability groups were scanned in supine position with hips in extension using in vivo micro-computed tomography (VivaCT, Scanco, Switzerland; 21 μm voxel, 55kVp, 145 μA, 100 msec integration time) prior to injury and at 2-, 6-, and 8-weeks post-injury under isofluorane anesthesia. The region of interest selected for imaging included bilateral hip joints. 3D CT images were exported as DICOM files for image processing. Acetabular coverage of the proximal femur was measured by using previously described methods.29 Briefly, hips were digitally repositioned and reoriented using the 3D MPR tool in OsiriX (64-bit software, Pixmeo, Geneva, Switzerland). Norberg angles (α) were evaluated for both hips in frontal plane.
At 8-weeks post-injury (11-weeks of age), mice (Moderate, n = 8; Severe, n = 7; FHR, n = 7) and age-matched naïve control mice (N = 6) were euthanized and tissues of the hip joint complex were collected intact with surrounding musculature removed for higher contrast imaging using ex vivo microCT (microCT40, Scanco, Switzerland; 20 μm voxel size, 70 kVp, 114 μA, 200 ms integration time). 3D CT images were exported as DICOM files and analyzed using CTan (Bruker, Kontich, Belgium) and FIJI/ImageJ.40 Using CTan, regions of interest (ROI; pelvis, proximal femur, and acetabular joint space) were manually traced based on anatomical landmarks, with interpolation between every 10th slice. Landmarks were chosen that were consistent across all specimens (Supplemental Fig. S1). The number of slices per ROI varied as size and distance between chosen landmarks differed between samples. The ROI used to determine pelvis bone morphometry were defined as the entire tissue volume of the acetabulum, beginning at 20 consecutive slices proximal to the femoral head (FH), and continued distally until the connection of the ischium to the pubis bone was observed. The ROI for the proximal femur included the entire volume of the FH and neck. The intertrochanteric line was used as the distal boundary of the femoral neck, and the greater and lesser trochanters were excluded from femoral neck measurements. Acetabular joint space was measured from the proximal acetabular rim to the acetabular notch using a contour of the lunate surface, acetabular fossa, and an interpolated plane of the acetabular rim in consecutive CT images. The volume of the acetabular joint space inside this plane was measured using BoneJ's Moments 3D plugin.41 Visualization of morphology of each traced region was performed using 3D reconstructions generated in OsiriX MD 8.0.1 (Pixmeo, Switzerland) and MeshMixer (Autodesk, California, version 3.0). Gaussian blur (kernel: square, radius: 0.5), unsharp mask (kernel: square, radius: 2, amount: 200, threshold: 0), multi-level Otsu thresholding (levels: 5, threshold: level of class), and adaptive thresholding (type: median, kernel: square, radius: 4, threshold: 90–255) were performed to segment bone from background noise prior to quantitative analysis of total volume (TV), bone volume (BV), bone volume fraction (BV/TV), and acetabular joint space volume (JSV). TV and acetabular JSV were measured as all pixels inside the contour (and excluding pixels in contact with contour line).


 Dynamic Histomorphology
Bone formation and morphology were assessed for an additional subset of mice (N = 5; 4 males and 1 female) following 8 weeks of severe instability group. Age-matched littermates (N = 3, all females) were used as uninjured controls. Following instability surgery, mice were administered calcein green (10 mg/kg, Sigma–Aldrich, St Louis, Missouri) and alizarin complexone (30 mg/kg, Sigma–Aldrich) by intraperitoneal injection at 10 days and 3 days prior to death, respectively. Mice were euthanized 8 weeks after instability surgery. Right and left femurs were disarticulated from the joint and fixed in 10% formalin overnight and stored in 70% ethanol (4°C), until microCT scanning (microCT40, 20μm resolution, 70 kV, 114 mA, 200 ms integration). Samples were then dehydrated in serially increasing ethanol concentrations (70–100%) followed by xylene, and then infiltrated and embedded in polymethylmethacrylate. Three to five sections (85–105 μm thick) per femur were cut in the frontal plane using a microtome (Leica SP 1600, Buffalo Grove, IL). Sections were mounted on glass slides, ground to 30-40μm thickness, and imaged using a fluorescent microscope (Zeiss Apotome.2, Thornwood, NY) using green fluorescence protein (GFP) and TexasRed filters for calcein and alizarin visualization, respectively. Images were digitized, stitched in Zen2 software (Zeiss), and qualitatively assessed for bilateral bone formation.


 Histology and Immunohistochemistry
Following ex vivo microCT, hips were decalcified in formic acid solution (ImmunoCal, American MasterTech, Lodi, CA) for 48–72 h. After decalcification, tissues were processed for paraffin embedding and sectioned in frontal plane at 5-micrometer thickness. Sections were stained with safranin o/fast green with Weigert's hematoxylin for cartilage quality, cellular localization, and extracellular matrix morphology. Stained sections were imaged on Aperio AT2 ScanScope (Leica Biosystems, Wetzlar, Germany), and Aperio ImageScope (Leica Biosystems, Version 12.3.0.05056) was used to obtain 5× images of the entire hip joint and 20× images of the articular cartilage of the FH and acetabulum.
Immunolocalization of collagen type II (COL2) and matrix metalloproteinase-13 (MMP13) was also performed on paraffin sections. Three biological replicates from each group (mild, moderate, severe, and FHR) were used. Sections were blocked by 5% goat serum and incubated with primary antibodies (COL2: rabbit polyclonal, Abcam ab34712; MMP13: mouse monoclonal; Santa Cruz sc515284) at 1:100 dilutions. For negative controls, sections were incubated with blocking buffer without primary antibodies. Antibody detection was performed using a commercially available kit (DAB peroxidase substrate kit, Millipore). Sections were counterstained with Mayer's hematoxylin and imaged using light microscopy (Zeiss, Gena, Germany).


 Statistical Shape Modeling
Image volumes of bilateral mouse hips from the severe instability group (N = 7; 4 male and 3 females) were used for statistical shape modeling. The surfaces of the injured and contralateral femurs were segmented and reconstructed in Amira (FEI 5.6 Merignac, France). The surfaces were isotropically scaled to an equivalent volume with cubic 1 mm voxels (50×) and smoothed to remove segmentation artifacts. Surfaces were reflected, as necessary, to appear as right femurs and aligned using the iterative closest point algorithm implemented in Amira.
Distance transforms, which define the femur shape implicitly within an image volume, were generated for the scaled femur surfaces using preprocessing tools for ShapeWorks.42 Manual particle placement or the use of training shapes is often necessary in development of a statistical shape model, which can be subjective and time consuming. ShapeWorks, however, uses an iterative splitting and optimization routine to automatically place correspondence particles.42 The location of all 2,048 correspondence particles was limited to the proximal femur, as identified with a plane below the third trochanter for each femur.
Principal Component Analysis (PCA) was used to reduce the high-dimensional data of the correspondence particle locations to a smaller set of linearly uncorrelated components (modes) used to describe the variation of the shapes. The correspondence particle locations were also used to identify mean shapes for the injured and contralateral femurs. Mean shape surfaces were smoothed and decimated using algorithms in PreView (Version 1.18.1).43 Morphological differences between shapes were calculated as the distance between the mean shape of the severe instability and mean shape of the contralateral femurs.


 Statistical Analysis
For measurements that had repeated time points (e.g., in vivo microCT measurements), statistical comparisons of Norberg angle were compared between injured and contralateral hips using a mixed-model ANOVA, matching for both factors (repeated measure by limb and repeated measures for time at 2-, 6-, and 8-weeks) with Sidak's multiple comparisons using Prism 6.0h (Graphpad, LaJolla, CA). For measurements that were taken at single time points (e.g., ex vivo microCT; JSV), a mixed-model ANOVA was used with repeated measure by limb (injured vs. contralateral) and between injury groups (naïve control, mild, moderate, severe, and FHR). Comparisons of JSV, proximal femur BV, TV, and BV/TV, and pelvis BV, TV, and BV/TV at 8 weeks post-injury only from ex vivo microCT measurements were compared using mixed model ANOVA (two-way ANOVA with repeated measures in one factor; left/right limb) with Sidak's multiple comparisons tests.
For statistical shape modeling, principal component loading values were evaluated to determine the significant (non-spurious) modes of variation using parallel analysis. Hotelling's T-squared test was used to evaluate pairwise group differences between injured and contralateral femurs, with the null hypothesis that the femurs were drawn from the same population. Modes of variation which represented significant variation between the injured and contralateral femurs were determined used a Student's T-test. Statistical analysis for evaluation of shape variation was completed in R.44

## RESULTS

Acetabular Coverage Patterns and Hip Morphometry
The pups tolerated the instability surgery well, with one fatality at time of operation caused by prolonged anesthesia (mild instability group, female). Mild instability did not lead to noticeable changes in FH coverage (Fig. 2A), Norberg angle (Fig. 3), or qualitative bone shape at 2-, 6-, or 8-weeks post-injury. Therefore, no additional mice were used in this study for the mild instability group. Additionally, for simplicity, data from the mild instability group are not shown. For mice subjected to moderate instability, lateralization of the proximal femur (Fig. 2B) with increased Norberg angle compared to contralateral side (Fig. 3) was observed. Norberg angle of the contralateral side of moderate instability hips was significantly decreased compared to naïve controls (Fig. 3). For mice subjected to either severe instability or FHR, Norberg angle and FH coverage were not calculated due to complete loss of FH in the acetabulum (Fig. 2C and D, respectively).

Moderate destabilization did not lead to noticeable morphological changes to the proximal femur or pelvis at 8-weeks post-injury (Fig. 4A). After 8 weeks of severe instability, however, the proximal femur in the injured hip had relatively more mature bone, indicated by fused growth plates, compared to the sham-operated, contralateral side (Fig. 4B). Additionally, severe instability resulted in closure of the acetabulum in the pelvis with rounded acetabular rim (Fig. 4B). Proximal femurs of the FHR group were non-existent following surgical removal and the remaining femoral neck was rounded and mineralized (Fig. 4C). The pelvis demonstrated a similar morphology of acetabular closure and rounded acetabular rim compared to the severe instability group (Fig. 4C).

Pelvis TV was significantly increased following FHR compared to naïve control hips, FHR contralateral hips, and injured hips with moderate instability (Fig. 5A). However, no significant differences in pelvis TV of the acetabular region were observed between injured and contralateral pelvises for either moderate or severe injury groups (Fig. 5B). Pelvis BV/TV following FHR was significantly reduced compared to the contralateral FHR pelvis and control naïve hips (Fig. 5C), and the contralateral pelvis of the FHR group had significantly higher BV/TV compared to contralateral pelvises of both the moderate and severe instability groups (Fig. 5C). Loss of the femoral head resulted in reduced proximal femur TV for the FHR injured hips compared to contralateral hips and injured hips following severe instability (Fig. 5D). However, no differences in femur TV were observed for moderate or severe hips compared to their paired contralateral sides (Fig. 5D). For the FHR group, BV was also significantly decreased in proximal femur compared to contralateral hips (Fig. 5E), and a significant decrease in proximal femur BV was measured in injured FHR hips compared to the same region in hips following severe instability (Fig. 5E). No significant differences were observed in proximal femur BV/TV between or within any groups at 8-weeks post-injury (Fig. 5F).

Acetabular JSV was significantly reduced at 8-weeks post-injury for both severe instability and FHR compared to contralateral hips and naïve control hips (Supplemental Fig. S2).  Microscopic Hip Joint Changes
No distinguishable histological differences were observed between contralateral and injured hips for mild instability, and cartilage looked healthy and intact on both the femoral head and acetabulum (Fig. 6A and B). Moderate instability led to increased rate of formation of the secondary ossification center, compared to contralateral hips (Fig. 6C and D). Articular cartilage of the femoral head and acetabulum appeared intact and healthy in both contralateral and moderate instability hips (Fig. 6C and D). Severe instability led to abnormal femoral head and trochanter shape, as well as formation of a pseudoacetabulum (Fig. 6F). In hips subjected to severe instability, fibrocartilage growth occurred on the proximal diaphysis/lesser trochanter of the femur, at points where the pelvis and femur were in contact (Fig. 6F). This cartilage growth appeared to result in fused contact surfaces between pseudoacetabulum and femur; accumulation of positive red staining indicated deposition of sulfated glycosaminoglycans (Fig. 6F). Additionally, cells at this interface had a rounded, fibrochondrocyte-like morphology (Fig. 6F). FHR led to formation of articular cartilage on the remaining femoral neck, which also appeared to widen with loss of the femoral head (Fig. 6H). FHR hips developed thickened articular cartilage and pseudoacetabulum (Fig. 6H). Heterogeneous distribution of red stain, indicating sulfated proteoglycan, was also present at the femur/pseudoacetebular interface (Fig. 6H). Contralateral hips for moderate, severe, and FHR hips appeared normal with intact cartilage, rich proteoglycan stain, and smooth articular surfaces (Fig. 5A, C, E, and G). Following 8-weeks of severe destabilization, the femoral head appeared less mature and less mineralized compared to the contralateral femur, as shown using dynamic histomorphometry (Supplemental Fig. S3).

 Changes in Proximal Femur Shape
For statistical shape modeling, the first five modes of variation from PCA accounted for 90% of the population variation; however, only the first three were determined to be significant. These first three modes accounted for 80.1% of the variation, with the first mode containing 47.2%, the second 24.2%, and the third 8.8% of the total variation. Only mode three was significantly different between the two groups (p = 0.02), which represented the variation in neck shaft angle and sphericity of the femoral head, as well as prominence of the lesser trochanter. The regions of greatest difference corresponded to the variations observed in PCA mode three (Fig. 8).

## DISCUSSION

Murine models have been used for over two decades to study the pathophysiology of knee OA.35 Yet our understanding of cartilage health and maintenance may be dependent on the anatomical environment and joint-specific considerations for joints other than the knee. To date, there is a lack of animal models that explore hip cartilage health,29, 45-47 and none that utilize surgical approaches for the induction of postnatal injuries. In this study, we have developed a new surgical injury model for improving understanding of the health and structure of the growing, postnatal hip. We show that soft tissue injury, including ligament transection and tenotomy, resulted in increased cartilage damage, fibrosis, and overall degenerative changes to the joint. The shape of the hip joint was severely affected by the detachment of soft tissue structures during postnatal growth, highlighting the critical role of soft tissue attachments and proper joint loading during the maturation of the hip.
This work focused on the role of destabilization on the mineralization, shape, and health of the growing postnatal murine hip. This work provides insight into how in vivo injury of the hip during postnatal growth can influence the health of the joint, cartilage, and bone. The development, validation, and implementation of a new mouse model to study hip destabilization during postnatal growth lends a novel new tool for identifying the adaptive response of the maturing joint to altered mechanical loading. In the future, this model will be useful for ascertaining the efficacy of therapeutic drugs, rehabilitation efforts, or localized deformations of the postnatal hip following altered mechanical loading. For example, known hallmarks of OA include increased MMP1348-50 and loss of COL2 in cartilage; these markers were differentially expressed in the joint tissues in this study depending on severity of injury. In our titrated model of postnatal hip dysplasia, MMP13 was primarily localized to the contralateral hip; this suggests that injury may induce abnormal biomechanics in the non-injured hip, similar to the human scenario of hip dysplasia.51-54 It is worth noting that our analysis of MMP13 is a snapshot of time after injury and its localization may differ at an earlier or later time after injury. The severe instability group had less COL2 staining of the articular cartilage, suggesting late stage OA and loss of structural integrity.48
This is the first study of its kind to identify the role of various magnitudes of hip instability on the postnatal, growing mouse hip. This mouse model is a potential small animal analogue of post-natal hip dysplasia. In this study, we developed a model for the controlled destabilization of the hip that resulted in lateralization of the femoral head without dislocation (i.e., moderate destabilization) or severe instability and dislocation (i.e., severe destabilization), with controllable and measurable morphological changes to the proximal femur and acetabulum.
There is ample room for improvement in the clinical management and potential circumvention of hip dysplasia. Acetabular dysplasia is a perplexing problem for clinicians both in terms of managing the unstable hip itself, as well as managing the resultant OA. An animal model such as ours can be leveraged to identify the biologic and mechanical underpinnings of cartilage health following hip destabilization and represents a potential opportunity for the identification of novel diagnostic and treatment modalities. Our animal model may aid in identifying more reliable biomarkers or radiographic abnormalities of the hip that can be used in newborn screening to preserve a critical window for intervention.
This is the first study to quantitatively model the shape of the proximal femur along with quantitative comparisons between normal development and development with morphological deformity in the murine hip. Using statistical shape modeling approaches, we found that the regions of largest shape variation following severe destabilization were localized to the lesser trochanter and intertrochanteric line, and these regions do not directly align with muscle insertion sites that would have been affected by the severe instability injury. Accordingly, alterations in loading due to instability within the joint likely have a more complex effect on bone morphology during development. Previous work by Chan et al. has shown that shape variations exist during normal growth in human patients with morphological deformities of the hip, such as Legg-Calvé-Perthes disease and slipped capital femoral epiphysis.55 Work by this group also demonstrated that the growth and shape attainment of the distal femur are comparable between humans and mice.56 Our present work provides a platform for understanding the progression and development of proximal hip deformities in a small animal model that mimic morphological deformities observed in the clinic, such as LCP, SCFE, and developmental dysplasia.
With the development of this model, we will now be able to explore the role of mechanical loading via surgical hip instability on hip morphological adaptations during post-natal growth and mineralization.

 Limitations
Although this model was developed for the induction of hip injury, we did not quantify the magnitude of instability that was induced following soft tissue injury. The effect of this soft tissue injury on long-term stability of the joint may prove relevant for ascertaining the healing and mechanical loading of the hip joint in vivo. For well-established small animal models of knee OA, the quantitative assessment of anterior-posterior and varus–valgus translation are commonly used as methods for assessing stability and laxity of the knee joint.57, 58 However, at this time, no such established methods exist for assessing hip joint laxity or for the quantitative measure of hip joint stability for small animal models. Future development of methods for assessing hip joint instability would benefit the field of hip biomechanics and could be used to identify the joint-level health of the developing or injured hip. A second limitation to this study is that, due to the extent of morphological changes observed after severe instability injury, generalizations of shape variability with such a small sample size may not be appropriate. However, the insight provided does indicate morphological changes and motivates future research.
